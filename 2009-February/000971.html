Markus, --

> The motivation is to have a full flexible system.

I see.
Flexibility and stability are antonyms,
at least in common sense ;)

> We had that system before and it turned out to be not
> flexible enough.

Such kind of reason I've suspected,
all right.
While stability and security were not on the table,
design decision made seems adequate.

My focus was sort of opposite, paranoid:
"what could we do to disable any possible tampering
of image content on the system run?"

Obvious first reaction is: read-only root file system.
I knew that unionfs encourages altering of
system files by it's design: top layer is always read-write.
Even if aufs allows read-only layer on the top of union,
this option is not used in kiwi.
Therefore only split image type left then
as the candidate for read-only root.
Now I see, split image won't fit security needs,
because of different point.
As of 11.4.5 section in kiwi.pdf:
"Unfortunately all common compressed filesystems
provides only read-only access."

Thanks to your detailed explanations, we can conclude that
read-only part of image had been considered as
a media restriction, rather then opportunity
for improve system security.

In this case, it may worth to not mess up
with two opposite requirements, but rather
 * invent new type of image, or
 * adjust one of other type images to
include option of read-only root, or something similar,
such as option "immune".

> I think that we have both a full flexible system but also at
> least a standard system working of no split information is there.

Agreed,
I end up my image build with similar conclusion.
There are little changes made in linuxrc
I could send you for certainty in further discussion,
out of kiwi-users list, if you don't mind.
